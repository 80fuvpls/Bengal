The album HEROES & VILLAINS by Metro Boomin became the emotional inspiration for this project-sparking waves of intensity that shaped its core. Yet beyond that initial trigger, the work is grounded in my own observations of society, spanning both digital spaces and lived reality.

HEROES OR VILLAINS (HOV) unfolds within a world overshadowed by darkness, where violence and indifference quietly persist. People choose not to see, allowing fear to guide them - symbolized through red blindfolds, the color of both blood and tears shed in silence. And yet, somewhere within that darkness, resistance begins to stir. Society, in its contradiction, challenges the rise of a “hero without a cape” - a figure it both desires and fears. When he appears, he is first celebrated, even worshipped, as humanity clings to the comfort of something extraordinary to believe in. But admiration soon turns into expectation; they begin to demand obedience, to shape him according to their own needs. When he refuses - choosing instead to preserve his clarity and autonomy - he is rejected, wounded, and ultimately redefined as a “villain.” And so, the cycle repeats. True heroes are cornered, misunderstood, and cast aside, while society continues to play the victim of the very figures it once elevated. In the end, he remains a hero - but only by his own definition, holding onto his ideals even if it means being seen as the antagonist.

HOV concludes with a quiet but unsettling reflection: perhaps it is humanity itself that ignites the flames of its own hell.

The idea for HOV emerged during a period of intense academic pressure, while the Photography Club was preparing for its first project of the 2024 - 2025 term. As Vice President and Creative Director of the club, I was responsible for initiating and leading this project, driven by both urgency and a need to find direction.
The creative phase paralleled with my preparation for final exams, making it difficult to balance structured thinking with artistic exploration. Additionally, the pressure of developing a concept for a 50-member club - especially as the first project of the term - limited the creative freedom I had previously experienced.
The project was successfully completed, producing strong visual results. More importantly, HOV fostered connection within the club, allowing both members and myself to experiment with new photographic techniques. It also established a creative foundation for over ten core members, shaping their approach moving forward.

Through HOV, I learned that growing up is learning to balance - between family and work, between study and creation, between what is seen and what is meant.
